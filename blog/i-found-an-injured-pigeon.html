I Found An Injured Pigeon

March 11, 2026

Identifying A Pigeon In Need

The first step to helping rescue a pigeon is to identify if the bird even needs your help in the first place. It's a common issue among not only pigeon rescues, but wildlife rescues in general, for birds to be taken into human care when it was never needed at all.

A pigeon likely needs your help if the following applies.

  • BEHAVIOR: Most pigeons will fly away when you get too close. If the bird is easy to approach and allows you to touch it, then it's likely in dire need of rescue. Some birds may also show some neurological damage, shown with twisted necks, inability to balance, and difficulty eating, drinking, or otherwise living a normal life.
  • AGE: Note if the bird looks like a fully grown adult, or if the bird is still yellow with baby fuzz. Younger pigeons may also have feathering, and look a little off from normal adult pigeons. Most pigeons are fine by the time they leave the nest. Pigeons also leave the nest and are able to eat mostly seeds by this age, but most leave the nest by four weeks old and stay within their parents' flock.
  • COLOR: Pigeons that are all white or mostly white may be in need of help. These are often ceremony release doves, which are really white pigeons. These birds stand out in the wild and often won't be able to join a flock on their own or take care of themselves.
  • INJURY: If the bird is unable to fly, is limping, has stringfoot, or appears to have open injuries, then it needs help.
  • BANDED: Bands are often placed on pigeons' for identification purposes. Most banded birds you see will be racing pigeons, with ceremony release and pets being the next. It's important to note that many banded birds may need help, those that join and incorporate into a flock often do not if they appear otherwise healthy.

Catching A Pigeon

If you've identified the pigeon needs help, your next step is to prepare and capture. If you have time before the catch, prepare a box or carrier with blankets or towels on the bottom for comfort and warmth. Do not be scared of pigeons. Their bites don't hurt very much if at all, and most pigeons won't bite or even wing slap you, but rather try to escape.

When you have the pigeon in your hands, secure it in your hands. Make sure to have a decent grip! Their feet can sometimes hurt if they get caught in your skin, allowing them to push themselves out. You can push the bird into your stomach or chest with one hand, and use your free hand as needed.

  • Docile birds or those unable to move can be caught with your hands.
  • Birds that allow you to get close could have a towel or shirt thrown over them.
  • Use either a crate or box trap to capture pigeons who are a bit more skittish, but still hungry.

For other methods to catch pigeons, we recommend checking out Palomacy Pigeon & Dove Rescue's article on "How to Catch a Pigeon or Dove in Need of Rescue."

Finally, there are a lot of myths about pigeons, hence why people are often scared of catching them, with the main concern being biosecurity and the risk of catching a disease or illness from pigeons. While some diseases carried by pigeons can be spread to other birds, they are seldom spread to humans. In fact, there were only 176 documented transmissions of illness from feral pigeons to humans between 1941 and 2003 (Haag-Wackernagel, D., & Moch, H., 2004). That's 176 people in 62 years, or ~3 people per year. Additionally, the biggest risk of contraction falls from inhaling pigeon droppings, which can be quite dusty, especially when cleaned at large. Routine cleaning poses little risk (New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, n.d.).



Temporary Care

All pigeons should be transferred to a rehabilitator with experience caring for pigeons. Here, they'll have the best chances at getting the bird veterinary care and consistent treatment without harm from the finder. Unless prescribed and told by a vet or rehabber, DO NOT GIVE THE PIGEON ANY SORT OF MEDICATION. Contact the closest rescue to you, or utilize the Palomacy Help Group for Pigeon & Dove Rescue & Adoption. This is an international Facebook group. Include your local area and any information you have about the bird. There are many independent rescuers out there who rehabilitate rock pigeons, not just rescues themselves.

Ensure the pigeon within the container stays inside of the container, but keep it in a dark, quiet spot, preferably where it's warm. You can also use a heating pad on the underside of the container, set on low. Provide a dish of water, preferably somewhat deep. Pigeons, unlike other birds, use their beaks and mouths as a straw. They do not scoop water and tilt their head up to drink. You can also provide a shallow dish of bird seed. In an emergency, any sort of bird seed works.

Injured or orphaned babies have different protocol. Do not try to feed the baby yourself. Place a heating pad underneath but only on one half of the box, so the baby can regulate temperature. Pigeons younger than two weeks old may have difficulty with seeds and water. We recommend calling a wildlife hospital helpline for assistance or following the advice of a pigeon rehabilitator who can aid you more closely with young pigeons.
