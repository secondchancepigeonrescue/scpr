Are Pigeons Right For You

March 9, 2026

Introduction

Pigeons are known to have a variety of personalities between individuals and even within the flock. Those who have pet pigeons currently have nearly all been misled by some sort of myth in the pet pigeon community.

Before bringing your future pet pigeon home, it's important to take all considerations into hand. Pigeons have different needs than other birds, different behaviors, and different long-term care. Even so, this information varies from pigeon to pigeon based on their preferences. Pigeon care requirements are often overlooked, but the commitment of time and resources is vital to caring for them.

Pigeons also thrive on routine and a stable environment. Veterinary care, housing, a balanced diet, and daily enrichment of some sort are required to keep them happy and healthy. This post is designed to help potential caretakers evaluate if a pet pigeon is the right choice for their home. We want everyone to make informed decisions, not impulsive ones.

Considerations

  • Personalities differ in pigeons, regardless of where they came from. Some breeders claim their birds have consistent personalities, being affectionate and sweet, and there are many rescues who take months to warm up. But the reverse also often happens, with birds from breeders being skittish for weeks or months, and rescue birds being sweet and affectionate within days. Not all pigeons are the same when it comes to preference and personality!

  • Pigeons are messy and dusty. Air purifiers are a must for this reason when owning pigeons. Those who have respiratory issues such as allergies or asthma, or something more serious, may not be able to have pigeons without additional air purifiers for health concerns. In addition, pigeons molt essentially year-round, with a few bigger molts throughout the year, increasing the need for cleaning. Pigeons also throw seed around much more than you'd think!

  • Pigeons need adequate time for both free roam and interaction. Free roaming is indoors ONLY. This does not apply to birds housed in properly sized outdoor aviaries. With our advice of a 42-inch dog kennel minimum, this space provides enough for most of the day outside of those 4+ hours of free roam recommended for pets.

  • Pigeons aren't family pets. Pigeons usually bond with a singular bird or person, rather than bonding to multiple members of the family. They may even go as far as to chase and bite others they don't view as their mate.

  • Pigeons may show a range of behavior if they bond to you. This includes some romantic behavior, including being nesty, wanting cuddles, and wanting to be in your space. Unfortunately, that includes mating behavior. Male birds will hump and often ejaculate on their owners. Some birds may playfight, which includes a lot of biting!

  • Pigeons are difficult to take over country borders. Many places have strict regulations on bringing birds from other countries, and pigeons often have to be shipped while being considered poultry.

  • Pigeons are quieter than some birds, but they can still be noisy. Pigeons don't scream or make shrill noises as parrots do, but coos can be loud, constant, and many times, you can't get it to stop despite your best efforts.

  • Pigeons can't be trained by punishment. Screaming, yelling, hitting, etc. don't register in pigeons' brains as them doing something bad. They can only be trained by positive reinforcement.

  • Pigeons cannot be with other pets. Dogs, cats, ferrets, and other pets all pose a predatory risk for pigeons. Parrots and even other dove species cannot be kept with pigeons due to behavioral differences.

  • With pairs, if you get two birds from two different sources, they may not be compatible. You may have to rehome one or both birds if it doesn't work out.

  • With pairs, double the bird and double the mess! Pigeons are already messy on their own. Two birds doubles this mess, and you'll stll have to clean just as much if not more with pairs.

  • For same-sex pairs, you need to replace eggs with fakes. You cannot stop pigeons, even single hens bonded to you, from producing eggs. You need to replace the eggs with fake eggs, and throw the real ones away. If you can't handle this, don't get a same-sex pair.
