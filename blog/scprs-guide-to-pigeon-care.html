SCPR's Guide To Pigeon Care

March 9, 2026

Introduction

Pigeons are among one of the most historically significant and widely misunderstood birds that the human race has domesticated. Our current domesticated pigeons have the same common ancestor, the rock pigeon (Columba livia). These birds were native to numerous areas in Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, and South Asia. According to Shapiro and Domyan, it was likely that humans used pigeons as utility birds for a food source as early as the Pleistocene in the Fertile Crescent around 10,000 years ago, though it isn't known if this confirms them being domesticated this far back or if they were hunted consistently. It is also noted by Shapiro and Domyan that fancy breeds were developed anywhere from 2000 years ago to now.

However, one known consistent instinct recognized among the wild rock pigeon is their innate homing instinct. For millennia, they served as messengers. This is heavily noted in the history regarding the World Wars by major forces. Among these includes G.I. Joe, a USA owned pigeon, arguably the most popularly recognized carrier pigeon in World War II, for saving over 1,000 British troops in 1943 when he delivered a message to cancel an Allied air raid as planes were preparing. He was the first non-British recipient of the Dickin Medal. Another is Mary of Exeter from the United Kingdom, who also recieved a Dickin Medal. She was wounded severely and still delivered a message.

During World War II however, new technological developments pushed pigeons to the side. We no longer had a use for them. Many were killed or abandoned after the war ended. Those that continued to breed and train homing pigeons devloped pigeons with their weight in gold and more. Pigeon racing became a popular sport, with price purses ranging up to thousands of dollars. However, pigeon racing has been studied to be more harmful than anything. The risk of predation is among one of the largest factors, with a majority of birds being lost by these means. Other causes include being disoriented, colliding with powerlines or other objects, or poisoning.

As pigeon owners, pigeon lovers, and for rescuers, all of which dedicating their time to learning more about these animals, it's our duty to provide the best possible care we can. This does not stay with only advocating, but by practicing the advice we give to others. Pigeons are our responsibility. Advocating for them starts with learning about what they need.

Do understand that while we do have studies on pigeons, many of those studies study specifically racing pigeons and consider pigeons in a livestock manner, rather than as pets. This leads to some nuance and calculating for pet pigeon owners. Much information discussed by the pet pigeon community is often anecdotal. In addition, there are pet pigeon influencers who have in the past spread misinformation regarding pet pigeon care, which many took as fact and parroted such information and used it to base their outlook on caring for pigeons. Do your research. You use any advice, including ours, at your discretion.

Diet and Nutrition

Seeds

Pigeons are granivores, and require a nutritionally balanced seed-based diet. In addition, a complete multivitamin and mineral supplement is required in order to keep your pigeon healthy. Most pigeon feeds will be found in feed stores, not pet stores.

We recommend to start out with 2 tbsp fed each morning for a 400 gram bird. Your bird may need more if they're eating all of their food by early evening, and may need less if they have more leftover by the time you're going to bed. If fed too much food, pigeons will often pick out the fattier seeds, leaving behind the rest after they're full and leading to an imbalanced diet.

Our recommended nutritional ratio is as follows:

  • Protein: 12-15%
  • Fat: 2-5%
  • Fiber: 3-6%

Pigeon seeds will contain some mixture of grains, legumes, and oil seeds. Among these, wheat, barley or sorghum will be added for grains. Peas, lentils, vetch, or soybeans will be added for legumes. For oil seeds, safflower or flax seeds are appropriate, with safflower being the most common. The mixture of these create a balanced diet when properly mixed, hence why we implore for your bird to be eating most of their food by the end of the day.

We do advise to avoid any feed mixes with corn or soybeans. Corn on its own is often considered a "filler food" for pet pigeons, and the fat is too high compared to other seeds. Additionally, soybeans are known to cause issues in pigeons due to the presence of isoflavones. Isoflavones are present in soybeans, and are classified as phytoestrogens, which can interact with estrogen receptors, with some studies reporting increased egg laying. It's important to note that many of these studies vary in result.

The following seed brands are recommended to those in the United States. Check nutritional contents before buying to ensure they're right for indoor pets as listed above. Below are some brands and specific mixes we can recommend within these guidelines. Note that seed mixes can go slightly above of these parameters, but we do not recommend going below them.

  • Versele-Laga (Show Standard without Maize, Classic 15% No Corn Mix)
  • Des Moines (No Corn Special Mix)
  • Brown's (Developer Popcorn)
  • Foy's


Multivitamins & Grit

There are two things we recommend for pigeons in addition to seeds. That's multivitamins and grit, which are dosed 2-3 times per week. Despite being granivores, seeds don't complete a pigeon's nutritional requirements! Multivitamins deliver additional vitamins not present in seeds. Soluble grit is required as well. It's a myth that pigeons need insoluble grit to break down food. The main reason to feed pigeons soluble grit is for additional calcium and minerals.

In addition, hens require an additional calcium supplement for egg laying, in addition to a UVB light. Sunlight through a window does not count. Calcium supplements should be provided 1-3 times per week. UVB bulbs should be replaced every 8 to 12 months. Place the light on top of the cage, and provide ~12 hours of light per day.

Note that some pigeon guides may say that a multivitamin is not required for birds that are recieving grit and seeds. This is completely false. Pigeons, especially laying hens, require multivitamins. Hens specifically need multivitamins in order to properly recover and produce eggs healthily, and lack of a multivitamin can cause egg binding.

Our current recommendations:

  • Multivitamins: Zoo Med Avian Plus, Nekton-S, Aviform Avigold
  • Soluble Grit: Des Moines Red Grit & Mineral Plus, Foy's Red Grit
  • Calcium Supplement (hens): Most avian calcium supplements are safe
  • UVB Lighting (hens) :Arcadia Bird PurseSun Mini/Midi


Treats

Treats are a great way to gain your birds trust and even train them. We recommend offering a few treats when you have time to try and bond with your bird. We also recommend around 1-2 tsp of treats per week. Recommended treats include safflower or shelled sunflower seeds. Your pigeon may have other preferences than these, however.



Water

Clean water should be provided daily and cleaned out regularly when messes are made. We recommend using filtered water. If you use tap or well water, a hardness no more than 100ppm should be used.

Housing

Basics of Housing

Pigeons should be housed in a cage no smaller than a size equivalent to a 42-inch dog kennel. This is recommended for those who can provide at least four hours of free roam time in a bird-proofed and predator-proofed room free of household dangers. There are, however, other alternative housing. Some choose to house their birds in outdoor aviaries in their yard or on their porch. In addition, we'll go over the requirements without a pigeon's enclosure to ensure they have enough space when you're gone.

Indoor Housing

Indoor Enclosure Options

  • Dog kennels/crates; 42-inches or greater
  • Parrot flight cages; 2ft x 3ft x 4ft or greater (larger preferred)
  • Large rabbit hutches
  • C&C panel cage (panels zip tied together)

Considerations

  • Predator-proofing. If you have cats, dogs, or parrots, the spacing between cage bars should not be large enough to allow paws, claws, or beaks through. Predators can be sneaky in opening cages and parrots are known to injure or be injured by pigeons. Use cages with narrow spacing or cover the cage sides with galvanized hardware cloth with 1/4 to 1/2 inch spacing, and around 16 gauge.
  • Flooring. Flooring should be flat, and made of something easily cleaned, such as wood, linoleum, or plastic. Wire flooring is innapropriate for pigeons. Other alternatives include puppy pads or washable blankets.
  • Cleaning. Spot cleaning should be done daily, with deeper cleans every 3-4 days. At least once a week, we recommend removing everything from the enclosure and deep cleaning it via a 1:1 ratio of white vinegar to water, and soaking items in bleachwater, rinsing, and air drying.
  • Perches. Offering flat perches is a must. These can be bought or crafted by DIYing. You can also offer natural branch perches or rope perches, but flat perches must be offered, as they compliment pigeons' behavioral preferences and anatomy.

Outdoor Housing

  • One recommendation by Palomacy (pigeon rescue) is a 10ft x 5ft x 6ft dog kennel or larger, which can fit around ten pigeons. It's required to wrap the entire cage in galvanized wire no less than 1/4 to 1/2 inch spacing, securing with zip ties, to ensure predator-proofing.
  • Premade aviaries are often not safe for pigeons. The Wingzstore aviaries are known to be safe.
  • Sizing. Aviaries should be at least 6 feet tall, with around 5-6 square feet per bird. An 8ft x 8ft x 6ft aviary can fit around a dozen birds.
  • Foundations can be built onto a wooden floor or the ground lined with hardware cloth or on cement. The entire mesh should be covered with wood, tile, or vinyl or not cement. Smooth and slightly sloped aids with drainage, but drainage can be built into some aviaries with the right space and work.
  • Predator proofing is achieved through the mentioned galvanized wire. All aviaries should be lined on the sides, top, and bottom, unless there's solid flooring/roofing that negates the need for it. Locks should also be predator-proofed.
  • Nest boxes should be provided for nesting birds. This is not for breeding, but for the birds to practice natural behaviors, as they are year-round layers, not seasonal.
  • Perches
    • should include flat wooden ones, which can include ramps from perch to perch. Natural branches work well in the outdoors and allow pigeons a variety, which aids in foot health.
Enrichment & Cage Decor

Bowls

Use washable bowls for seed and grit. We recommend one for water and one for seed, as grit and multivitamins are sprinkled on food or in water. Terracotta, porcelain, metal, or hard plastic make great bowls.

Toys & Foraging Enrichment

  • Foraging mats and dig boxes are great to sprinkle some seeds or treat seats, which allows them to display natural foraging behavior. You can fill dig boxes with things like crinkle paper or natural chews used for parrots, like large cork bark or coconut toys.
  • Cat crinkle toys, bell balls, or other toys work great for birds who enjoy throwing things around.
  • Natural bird toys are great for pigeons, though notably not every pigeon likes them.
  • Plush toys are good for pigeons to wrestle with or bond to.
  • Warning: though mirrors are often recommended for pigeons, we do not recommend them. Only some pigeons can recognize themselves in the mirror, and some birds may bond to their reflection.

Perches

  • Flat perches must be offered to pigeons, as it best compliments their natural behavior of prefering cliffs and ledges in the wild.
  • Natural branch perches offer variety in the enclosure.
  • Rope perches are good for additional variety. Watch for fraying in threads, as pigeons can ingest it and lead to blockages or other digestive issues.
  • Platforms or bricks can be provided, which pigeons also prefer.

Nesting & Nesting Materials

  • Nest boxes can be anything from 12 x 12 inch boxes, to cat beds or hides. Some also like large dog bowls.
  • Nesting materials are a bit of a picky topic with pigeons. Some prefer certain thicknesses or color of certain items. Regardless, we recommend q-tips, strips of paper, crinkle paper of varying colors, straw, hay, or coco fiber.

UVB Lighting

UVB lighting is essential for hens of laying age in order to supply vitamin D3 for laying. Egg binding can be caused otherwise. We recommend using the PureSun Mini or PureSun Midi, and placing it on top of the cage on one side, near a perch, so that your hen can self-regulate toward or away from the lighting. Around 12 hours of light is appropriate per day.
